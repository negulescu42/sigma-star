\begin{abstract}
A prediction that aggregates training points with distance-decaying weights depends, in principle, on every point. We give a label-free certificate that it depends only on the near ones: past a chosen radius $d$, the total influence of all sources beyond $d$ is provably below a budget $\varepsilon$. For a \emph{kernel field}---any superposition of distance-decaying kernels of common bandwidth $\sigma$---this yields a closed form, $\sstar = d/\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}$, with the interfering mass $A$ \emph{measured} from the field, not tuned. The mass is nondecreasing in $\sigma$, so one measurement is provably conservative. The same inequality certifies safety, decision stability, abstention, and data locality---the output is $\varepsilon$-insensitive to deleting or corrupting any far point. On frozen ResNet-18, ResNet-50, and ViT-B/16 fields over CIFAR-100, and on text and tabular fields, $\sstar$ holds the budget where naive, plug-in, and density-estimation bandwidths fail by two to five orders of magnitude, coming within $1$--$5$ points of a labelled grid search (about $6$ on low-dimensional tabular data) using zero labels. Because the mass is re-measured, the certificate re-certifies as the field grows: as a fixed memory densifies, across three backbones $\sstar$ tracks a per-stage re-tuned labelled oracle with zero labels, while a bandwidth frozen by a one-time search drifts to hundreds of times the budget on two of them---the adaptivity a one-time labelled search structurally lacks, since it cannot re-tune on a field it has not yet seen. The advantage is over that frozen comparator and follows densification, not class count. The construction needs only a monotone tail, not the Gaussian: a Laplace field calibrates at parity, and the conservativeness holds for every kernel whose log is concave in log-distance. The interfering mass is one member of a Rényi/Hill family whose order tunes tightness against smoothness, and a label-free conformal wrapper turns the average certificate into a finite-sample guarantee for the next query. For \emph{normalised} readouts---softmax attention and prototypical networks---a companion Gibbs certificate bounds the far probability mass directly in the logit gap, so the same calibration reaches distance-based deep readouts: a full locality guarantee for the bounded-weight prototype, and a computable, temperature-explicit far-mass bound for attention, single-head and across a full multi-head layer. The result is one inequality read at three levels---additive kernel fields, normalised Gibbs fields, and finite-sample certification of a future query. The bandwidth question, for a kernel field, is a calibration, not a search.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A predictor that reads a space through distance-decaying weights over stored points depends, in principle, on all of them: every stored point contributes to every query. We build such predictors expecting them to be \emph{local}---to answer from the near points and ignore the rest---but nothing in the construction guarantees it. This paper supplies that guarantee, in closed form and without labels, for one of the most reused constructions in applied mathematics: the \emph{kernel field}. Place weighted sources at points $z_i$ of a metric space, attach to each a kernel of common width $\sigma$, and read the field at a query $y$ as the superposition of the nearby kernels. Kernel density estimators, Nadaraya--Watson regressors, radial-basis interpolants (whose shape parameter is the same bandwidth choice~\cite{rippa1999}), Gaussian-process posteriors, and the local correction fields used to adapt frozen models are all instances of this one object, sharing one free parameter---the bandwidth $\sigma$, which sets the resolution at which the field reads the space. Too fine and it responds only at its sources; too coarse and it bleeds across the space and washes out the structure it was meant to resolve.

How is $\sigma$ chosen? Almost always by search: a grid of candidate values, a random sample, or a cross-validated sweep, each scored by instantiating the field and measuring an outcome. Every such method spends an \emph{outcome signal}---a held-out likelihood, a validation loss, a labelled accuracy---at every candidate, and none of them returns a guarantee about the value it selects.

We show the search is unnecessary, and that it asks the wrong question. The right question is not ``which $\sigma$ scores best on held-out labels'' but ``which $\sigma$ keeps the field's cross-source interference below a stated budget''---and that question has a closed-form, label-free answer. The \emph{certified frontier} $\sstar=d/\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}$ is the largest bandwidth certified by a conservative aggregate envelope obtained from one mass measurement---its aggregate interference bound past a radius $d$ is at $\varepsilon$, with the effective mass $A$ \emph{measured} from the field, not tuned (Section~\ref{sec:cert}). The exact tail admits a slightly larger bandwidth (our own label-free search finds $\approx\!1.1\times$; Section~\ref{sec:related}); $\sstar$ is the price of a closed form with an a-priori conservativeness proof. Its structural core is that this measured mass is nondecreasing in $\sigma$, so a single measurement is provably conservative: no search, no labels, and a closed-form, a-priori guarantee that an empirical tail search---which can match the mean frontier but returns no closed form and no per-query proof---does not supply. The one interference bound reads four ways at once---safety, decision stability, abstention, and data \emph{locality}---the last certifying that the prediction depends only on the training points within $d$ (Section~\ref{sec:cert}).

Because the certificate uses only that the output superposes distance-decaying weights, it is not confined to kernel density estimation. Softmax attention, though built from inner products rather than distances, reduces exactly to a Gaussian kernel field over its keys, and a prototypical network already is one; the certificate therefore transfers to both---bounding far-key interference in pretrained transformers and far-prototype interference in few-shot learners (Section~\ref{sec:reach}). On three frozen vision backbones and four further modalities it holds the budget where the label-free selectors we tested fail and matches a labelled grid search using zero labels (Section~\ref{sec:flagship}). And because the mass is re-measured rather than tuned, it re-certifies a field it has never labelled or tuned on: as a fixed memory densifies, $\sstar$ tracks a per-stage re-tuned labelled oracle with zero labels while a bandwidth frozen by a one-time search drifts to hundreds of times the budget (Section~\ref{sec:grow})---adaptivity against that frozen comparator that a one-time labelled search structurally lacks, in the regime of continually-updated models and growing memories.

\section{The certificate}\label{sec:cert}
\paragraph{Kernel fields.} Fix a metric space, taken here as $\Rbb^n$. A \emph{kernel field} is a finite collection $F=(z_i,v_i)_{i=1}^{M}$ of sources $z_i\in\Rbb^n$ carrying signed weights $v_i\in\Rbb$, together with a Gaussian kernel of common bandwidth $\sigma$, written in squared distance as
\begin{equation}
\Ksig(t)=\exp\!\big(-t/2\sigma^2\big),\qquad t=\lVert y-z_i\rVert^2 .
\end{equation}
The field's value at a query $y$ is the superposition $\sum_i v_i\,\Ksig(\lVert y-z_i\rVert^2)$. The base certificate controls this \emph{unnormalised} sum; the normalised readouts of Section~\ref{sec:reach} (softmax attention, prototype and Nadaraya--Watson fields) are covered separately by the Gibbs certificate proved there, which bounds normalised far mass directly. Density estimators, interpolants, and the correction fields of Section~\ref{sec:flagship} are all fields of this unnormalised form. Write $V_{\max}=\max_i|v_i|$; the certified quantity is invariant under $(v_i,\varepsilon)\mapsto(cv_i,c\varepsilon)$, so we fix $V_{\max}=1$ (the budget $\varepsilon$ is in units of the largest source weight), which holds exactly for the one-hot Parzen vote of Section~\ref{sec:flagship}.

\paragraph{Near and far, and the effective mass.} Around a query $y$, a separation radius $d$ partitions the sources: those within $d$ are \emph{near} (the field should respond to them), those in $\mathcal{F}=\{i:\lVert y-z_i\rVert>d\}$ are \emph{far} (it should leave them undisturbed). The far tail $\mathrm{Tail}_\sigma(y,d)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}|v_i|\,\Ksig(\lVert y-z_i\rVert^2)$ is the field mass leaking past $d$; the bandwidth question is how large $\sigma$ can be before it exceeds a budget $\varepsilon$. Not every far source matters equally: most are negligibly weak, and a handful near the radius carry the tail. The right count is therefore not $|\mathcal{F}|$ but the number of far sources that \emph{effectively} contribute---the participation ratio of the far weights $\omega_i=\Ksig(\lVert y-z_i\rVert^2)$, which equals the true count when they are equal and drops toward one when a few dominate,
\begin{equation}
\Neff(\sigma)=\frac{\big(\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}\omega_i\big)^2}{\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}\omega_i^2}\in[1,|\mathcal{F}|],
\end{equation}
which must be restricted to $\mathcal{F}$: a participation ratio over all sources is dominated by the near mass and does not bound the tail. The field mass acting past the radius is $A=V_{\max}\Neff\ge1$.

\paragraph{Assumptions.} The certificate rests on three explicit conditions. (A1) \emph{Bounded weights}: $|v_i|\le V_{\max}=1$. (A2) \emph{A meaningful near/far radius}: $d>0$ partitions sources into a near set the field should respond to and a far set it should not, so every far source satisfies $\omega_i\le\Ksig(d^2)$. (A3) \emph{Budget below mass}: $0<\varepsilon<A$. It is worth separating three kinds of statement in what follows: the frontier and the safety, stability, and conservativeness results are \emph{proven} under (A1)--(A3); the effective mass $A$ and the tail ratios are \emph{measured} from the field; and the radius $d$ (here the tenth percentile of pairwise centre distances) is a \emph{design choice}---any fixed rule gives a valid certificate.

\paragraph{The safety frontier.} Define the leakage certificate $\mathrm{cert}(A,\sigma,d)=A\,\Ksig(d^2)=A\,e^{-d^2/2\sigma^2}$.
\begin{proposition}[Safety frontier]\label{prop:frontier}
For $A>0$, $d>0$, $0<\varepsilon<A$, the equation $\mathrm{cert}(A,\sigma,d)=\varepsilon$ has the unique solution
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sstar}
\sstar(A,\varepsilon,d)=\frac{d}{\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}},
\end{equation}
and the safe set $\{\sigma>0:\mathrm{cert}\le\varepsilon\}$ is $(0,\sstar]$.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\emph{Sketch.} $\mathrm{cert}$ is continuous and strictly increasing in $\sigma$, rising from $0$ to $A$; solving $\mathrm{cert}=\varepsilon$ gives the unique frontier, and monotonicity makes the safe region $(0,\sstar]$ (SI Note~1). The single-source bound extends to the whole tail: because every far source lies beyond $d$, each far kernel is bounded by $\Ksig(d^2)$, and the participation-ratio mass converts this per-source bound into an aggregate one,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aggbound}
\mathrm{Tail}_\sigma(y,d)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}|v_i|\,\omega_i\le V_{\max}\,\Neff\,\Ksig(d^2)=\mathrm{cert}(A,\sigma,d),
\end{equation}
with $A=V_{\max}\Neff$; at $\sigma=\sstar$ the aggregate leakage is at most $\varepsilon$ (SI Note~1).

\paragraph{The measured mass is self-consistent.} The frontier depends on $A=V_{\max}\Neff(\sigma)$, but $\Neff$ itself depends on $\sigma$, so $A$ must be measured at some $\sigma$ and then used to set $\sstar$. We measure it once at the pairwise scale $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}=d/\sqrt{2\ln(1/\varepsilon)}$ (the frontier for a single far source) and deploy at $\sstar$. This one-shot procedure is provably conservative:
\begin{proposition}[Conservativeness]\label{prop:conserv}
$\Neff(\sigma)$ is nondecreasing in $\sigma$. Consequently $\sstar\le\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}$, the mass measured at $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}$ satisfies $A(\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}})\ge A(\sstar)$, and the true tail at the deployed bandwidth obeys $\mathrm{Tail}_{\sstar}(y,d)\le A(\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}})\,K_{\sstar}(d^2)=\varepsilon$.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\emph{Sketch.} As $\sigma$ grows the far weights become more uniform, so their participation ratio cannot decrease; since $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}\ge\sstar$, measuring the mass there overestimates the deployed mass, and the one-shot certificate holds (SI Note~2). On the three frozen backbones the one-shot mass over-estimates the deployed mass by $2.2$--$4.3\times$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:conserv}a) and the measured one-shot tail is $0.08$--$0.16\,\varepsilon$---strictly inside budget on every network. The exact self-consistent bandwidth, when wanted, is the unique root of the monotone envelope $A(\sigma)\Ksig(d^2)=\varepsilon$ and is found by bisection; the naive re-measurement iteration settles empirically in six to seven steps (Fig.~\ref{fig:conserv}b), but the one-shot value already certifies (SI Note~2). Both load-bearing facts---the participation-ratio tail bound and this monotonicity---are confirmed independently on random configurations (zero violations in $20{,}000$ and $3{,}000$ trials respectively; SI Note~8).

\paragraph{The same bound controls the decision.} Consider a field used as a classifier: each class $c$ receives a score $S_c(y)=\sum_{i:\,\mathrm{class}(i)=c} v_i\,\Ksig(\lVert y-z_i\rVert^2)$, with prediction $\arg\max_c S_c$. Splitting each score by the near/far partition, the certificate bounds the far part of every class score by $\varepsilon$ at $\sigma=\sstar$. Let the \emph{local prediction} be $\arg\max_c S_c^{\mathrm{near}}$ and the \emph{local margin} $m(y)$ the gap between the top two near-scores.
\begin{theorem}[Decision stability]\label{thm:stable}
If $\sigma\le\sstar$ and $m(y)>2\varepsilon$, the deployed prediction equals the local prediction: the far field cannot change the decision.
\end{theorem}
\noindent\emph{Sketch.} Each class score moves by at most $\varepsilon$ under far-field interference, so a two-class gap moves by at most $2\varepsilon$; a near margin above $2\varepsilon$ cannot be flipped (SI Note~3). This is the perturbation bound underlying interval-based certified robustness~\cite{gowal2018}; what is new is \emph{where $\varepsilon$ comes from}---a bandwidth calibrated so the far-field perturbation is provably at most $\varepsilon$, read from geometry without labels. The test uses only near scores and $\varepsilon$, so a deployer flags certified queries per query, and a floor follows:
\begin{corollary}[Performance floor]\label{cor:floor}
At $\sigma\le\sstar$, deployed accuracy $\ge$ (near-field accuracy) $-\ \Pr[\,m(y)\le 2\varepsilon\,]$.
\end{corollary}
\noindent The same inequality certifies \emph{data locality}. Let $F'(y)$ be the field with its far set $\mathcal{F}$ arbitrarily deleted, or replaced by any \emph{admissible} corrupted far set: sources of bounded weight $|v_i'|\le V_{\max}$ located outside the radius $d$ whose effective mass satisfies $\Neff'\le A$. (Corrupting the weights of the far sources in place leaves their positions, hence $\Neff$, unchanged and is always admissible; the condition only excludes an adversary who repositions far mass to inflate $\Neff'$ past the measured budget.)
\begin{corollary}[Far-set locality]\label{cor:locality}
At $\sigma\le\sstar$: (i) deleting the far set moves the output by at most the budget, $\lVert F(y)-S(y)\rVert=\lVert T(y)\rVert\le\varepsilon$; (ii) replacing it by any admissible far set shifts each class score by at most $\varepsilon$, so $\lVert F'(y)-F(y)\rVert\le2\varepsilon$; and (iii) if $m(y)>4\varepsilon$ the decision is unchanged under \emph{any} such deletion or corruption. The prediction is therefore $\varepsilon$-insensitive to the presence, and $2\varepsilon$-insensitive to the identity, of every training point beyond $d$.
\end{corollary}
\noindent\emph{Sketch.} (i) is Proposition~\ref{prop:frontier} verbatim: $\mathrm{Tail}_\sigma(y,d)\le\varepsilon$ \emph{is} $\lVert T(y)\rVert\le\varepsilon$. For (ii) each admissible far set has $\Neff'\le A$, so it contributes at most $V_{\max}\Neff'K_\sigma(d^2)\le V_{\max}A\,K_\sigma(d^2)=\varepsilon$ to any class score by the same certificate (the admissibility bound $\Neff'\le A$ is exactly what keeps the corrupted tail inside the budget); the original and corrupted far contributions each lie in a ball of radius $\varepsilon$ and differ by at most $2\varepsilon$; (iii) is then Theorem~\ref{thm:stable} applied to a $2\varepsilon$ perturbation. All three hold empirically on four datasets, and fail by two to three orders of magnitude at a bandwidth $3\times$ too wide (Fig.~\ref{fig:locality}).

\paragraph{One certificate, four readouts.} At $\sigma=\sstar$ every query splits the field as $F(y)=S(y)+T(y)$ with $\lVert T(y)\rVert\le\varepsilon$---which \emph{is} Proposition~\ref{prop:frontier}. Because $S(y)$ is precisely the response the field would give with the entire far set deleted, the same inequality reads four ways:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Safety}: the deployed response is within $\varepsilon$ of the near-field response, $\lVert F(y)-S(y)\rVert\le\varepsilon$.
\item \emph{Performance}: if the near margin exceeds $2\varepsilon$, $\arg\max F=\arg\max S$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:stable}).
\item \emph{Abstention}: if the top near score is $\le2\varepsilon$ the certificate cannot fire, and the query lies outside the field's certified support---a label-free per-query support signal.
\item \emph{Locality}: the output is $\varepsilon$-insensitive to the presence, removal, or corruption of \emph{every} far source---deleting the entire far set moves $F(y)$ by at most $\varepsilon$, and no admissible far set can flip a decision with near margin above $4\varepsilon$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:locality}). This is a measured, per-configuration data-locality guarantee: it certifies \emph{which} training points a prediction can depend on, and connects the bandwidth to unlearning and attribution~\cite{ginart2019,koh2017} rather than to density estimation. It is not a differential-privacy bound---there is no randomisation and the guarantee is over the field as measured, not worst-case over datasets---but it occupies the same role for distance-based predictors.
\end{itemize}
We confirmed the decomposition on $20{,}000$ certified-regime configurations with zero violations, and on a real frozen ResNet-18 field ($20{,}000$ kernels, $10{,}000$ queries) the certified $\sstar$ flags $27\%$ of queries stable with a stable-implies-equal rate of $100.00\%$---zero violations---while the naive bandwidth certifies nothing and its accuracy has collapsed. The certified-stable set sits on the field's accuracy--coverage curve rather than above it; its distinguishing property is that the selection rule is label-free and a priori (SI Note~6). A Lipschitz argument extends the pointwise bound to a ball: the certified radius is genuine but small ($\delta\approx1.6$--$2.2\%$ of inter-query distance), so the per-query certificate carries the deployment claims (SI Note~4).

\section{The certificate in practice}\label{sec:flagship}
The certificate was built for the dense regime, where many sources act at once and the naive single-interferer intuition fails. We reproduce that failure, and its cure, on real frozen deep networks.

\paragraph{Construction.} For each backbone---ResNet-18 ($512$-d), ResNet-50 ($2048$-d), and ViT-B/16 ($768$-d), all ImageNet-pretrained and frozen---we extract the penultimate features of CIFAR-100 and build a correction field of $20{,}000$ balanced kernels ($200$ per class) over the training features, classified by a kernel-weighted (Parzen) vote. The locality radius $d$ is the tenth percentile of pairwise centre distances---pure geometry, no labels. We fix $\varepsilon=0.05$ a priori and compare three bandwidths: the naive single-interferer rule $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}=d/\sqrt{2\ln(1/\varepsilon)}$; the certified frontier $\sstar$, whose mass $A=V_{\max}\Neff$ is \emph{measured} once at $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}$; and, as an oracle reference, a labelled grid search over a $30$-point log-spaced grid (denoted ``grid''). We also run the classical label-free density bandwidths---Silverman, Scott, and Abramson's square-root law.

\paragraph{Computing $\sstar$ (label-free).} Four steps, no labels: (1) fix $\varepsilon$ and set $d$ to the tenth percentile of pairwise centre distances; (2) form $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}=d/\sqrt{2\ln(1/\varepsilon)}$; (3) measure $A=V_{\max}\Neff$ at $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}$, averaging the far-mask participation ratio over queries; (4) return $\sstar=d/\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:conserv} measuring at $\sigma_{\mathrm{pair}}\ge\sstar$ over-estimates $A$, so one pass suffices. Full computational specification, grids, and seeds are in SI Note~7 and the repository.

\paragraph{Results.} The pattern is identical across all three architectures (Fig.~\ref{fig:multibb}, Table~\ref{tab:multibb}). The naive bandwidth overshoots the interference budget by three to four orders of magnitude (tail/$\varepsilon$ from $4.9\times10^3$ to $1.0\times10^4$) and collapses the field into a single component spanning \SIrange{97}{100}{\percent} of the centres. The certified $\sstar$ holds the budget (tail/$\varepsilon$ between $0.08$ and $0.16$), dissolves the giant component to \SIrange{4}{7}{\percent}, and recovers accuracy to within \SIrange{1}{5}{} points of the labelled grid-search optimum---using \emph{zero labels}, with $\varepsilon$ fixed across every backbone so the adaptation to $512$, $768$, or $2048$ dimensions happens automatically through the measured mass $A$. The classical selectors are not straw comparators and fail here: Silverman and Scott over-smooth catastrophically (tail/$\varepsilon>10^5$, accuracy \SIrange{27}{64}{\percent}, \emph{below} the naive rule), Abramson's rule worse still (\SIrange{12}{36}{\percent}). The two label-free distance heuristics---the median heuristic and a $k$-nearest-neighbour scale, the natural competitors to $\sstar$---pick a bandwidth $3.6$--$6\times$ larger, which drives tail/$\varepsilon$ to $\sim\!10^5$ and loses $9$--$26$ accuracy points: the quantile $d$ fixes the \emph{scale}, but the logarithmic factor $\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}$ is what turns it into a \emph{safe} one, and only the calibration supplies it. Over five re-samples the certified accuracy varies by $\le0.3$ points on every backbone ($56.3\pm0.2$, $51.1\pm0.2$, $72.2\pm0.2\,\%$), so the ordering is not seed-luck.

\paragraph{The certificate is robust to its own design choices.} Four checks, all label-free, confirm the operating point is not fragile (SI Note~5). \emph{From average to per-query.} The mass $A$ is a query-average, so the certificate is stated on the average tail; in deployment one cares about the \emph{worst single query}. Empirically the two coincide: over $10{,}000$ ResNet-18 queries the largest single-query tail/$\varepsilon$ is $0.24$, still well inside budget, so no query exceeds it. To make the per-query guarantee a priori rather than empirical, set $A$ to an upper quantile of $\Neff$ instead of its mean; this costs $\le0.2$ points of accuracy and gives zero exceedances on all three backbones. The near set is $25$--$47\times$ class-enriched (weighted near purity $0.31/0.25/0.47$ against a chance level of $0.01$), though this is a \emph{plurality}: the certificate guarantees \emph{which} sources decide, not that they decide correctly, and accuracy remains a separately measured outcome. And accuracy stays flat across the $\varepsilon\times$percentile grid ($\le6.7$ points, over a realized $\sstar$ range of only $1.34$--$1.41\times$; SI Note~5) while the certified tail stays in budget---a conjunction that a merely accuracy-tuned dial would not show, since a bandwidth $4$--$6\times$ larger fails catastrophically.

\paragraph{Beyond vision: four non-image modalities.} The certificate is a property of the kernel-field geometry, not of the image domain. We test it on four corpora unrelated to CIFAR (Table~\ref{tab:modality}, Fig.~\ref{fig:modality}), each over five seeds: three text collections---DBpedia, AG-News, Yahoo-Answers---embedded with a frozen sentence transformer ($384$-d), and a raw tabular set (Covertype, $54$-d). The naive bandwidth overshoots by $3.6\times10^2$ to $2.3\times10^3$ while $\sstar$ holds tail/$\varepsilon<1$, and every stable-flagged query has its deployed prediction equal to the intended one ($100\%$ on every corpus). On the three high-dimensional text sets $\sstar$ matches the labelled grid search to within $0.9$ points \emph{label-free}; on the low-dimensional tabular set it trails by $5.8$ points, the same specificity-first behaviour as the public datasets. The pattern the flagship establishes on vision reproduces, with error bars, on text and tabular data alike.

\paragraph{Beyond the Gaussian: monotone, log-concave tails.} The certificate uses the Gaussian shape only lightly, and the two halves need different amounts. The frontier itself---inverting $A\,K_\sigma(d)=\varepsilon$---needs only that the far weight $\omega_i=K_\sigma(\lVert y-z_i\rVert)$ be monotone and invertible in $\sigma$. One-shot conservativeness needs more: the normalised far-weight vector must flatten in majorization order as $\sigma$ grows, which holds exactly when $\ln K$ is concave as a function of $\ln$ distance (SI Note~2). So any invertible monotone tail admits a frontier; the log-distance-concave kernels additionally admit conservative one-shot mass measurement. The Laplace kernel $K_\sigma(r)=e^{-r/\sigma}$ is one such: its frontier is $\sstar=d/\ln(A/\varepsilon)$---the same radius-over-a-log-factor structure, with the square-root gone because the tail is exponential in $r$ rather than $r^2$---and its far weights still flatten as $\sigma$ grows, so $\Neff$ is nondecreasing and one measurement still over-estimates. We verify this on iris, wine, breast-cancer, and digits (Fig.~\ref{fig:kernels}): the Laplace $\sstar$ holds tail/$\varepsilon<1$ on all four (mean $0.16$--$0.24$), matches a labelled grid search anchored to its own scale within $0$--$2.8$ points, and shows zero monotonicity violations. The principle is a property of log-concave-tail kernel fields, not of the Gaussian shape alone.

\section{The certificate reaches attention and prototype networks}\label{sec:reach}
The certificate uses only that a prediction is a superposition of distance-decaying weights over stored points. That description is not special to density estimation: it fits two of the most widely used readouts in modern deep learning, one of which is not obviously a kernel field at all. We show both inherit the certificate, and we verify it on real trained networks.

\paragraph{The normalised certificate.} The base frontier (Section~\ref{sec:cert}) bounds the \emph{unnormalised} tail $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}v_i\Ksig(\cdot)$. Softmax attention, prototypical networks and Nadaraya--Watson regression are instead \emph{normalised} Gibbs fields, $p_i=e^{-E_i/\tau}/\sum_j e^{-E_j/\tau}$, where the output-relevant quantity is the normalised far mass $m_{\mathcal{F}}=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{F}}p_i$; deleting the far set rescales the denominator, so the unnormalised bound does not transfer verbatim. A companion certificate bounds $m_{\mathcal{F}}$ directly: with a near anchor at minimal energy and a far set separated by an energy gap $\Delta$, $m_{\mathcal{F}}\le D_q^{\mathcal{F}}(\tau)\,e^{-\Delta/\tau}$. Since $D_q^{\mathcal{F}}$ is itself temperature-dependent, the executable form mirrors the additive one-shot theorem: measure the Hill number $D_{q,0}$ once at a reference temperature $\tau_0$, and deploying at $\tau_{\mathrm{cert}}=\min(\tau_0,\,\Delta/\ln(D_{q,0}/\varepsilon))$ guarantees $m_{\mathcal{F}}\le\varepsilon$ (Theorem~2, SI Note~13; $D_q^{\mathcal{F}}$ is the Rényi/Hill effective mass of the far weights, of which the participation ratio $\Neff$ is the $q{=}2$ member, SI Note~12). This has no source-amplitude factor---it is exactly the unbounded-$V_{\max}$ obstruction that made attention the weakest case above, now removed---and for a prototypical network ($E_i=\lVert f(x)-c_i\rVert^2$) and softmax attention ($E_i=-\langle q,k_i\rangle$) it holds exactly. The two views are complementary: the additive frontier certifies the interference \emph{tail} in output units; the Gibbs certificate certifies the normalised far \emph{mass} that a softmax reads.

\paragraph{Beyond kernel density: a transformer's attention.} The predictors above are all built as kernel fields by construction. Softmax attention is not---its weight on key $i$ is $\exp(\langle q,k_i\rangle/\tau)$, an inner product, not a distance. Yet the polarisation identity $\langle q,k_i\rangle=\tfrac12(\lVert q\rVert^2+\lVert k_i\rVert^2-\lVert q-k_i\rVert^2)$ makes the query-only factor cancel in the softmax, leaving \emph{exactly} a Gaussian kernel field over the keys with bandwidth $\sigma=\sqrt{\tau}$ and per-source weight $v_i=e^{\lVert k_i\rVert^2/2\tau}$ (SI Note~9). This algebraic identity is what lets a distance-based certificate reach attention at all; but because the induced weights $v_i(\tau)=e^{\lVert k_i\rVert^2/2\tau}$ themselves vary with $\tau$, the additive frontier does not transfer as a fixed-temperature deployment guarantee, and the operational attention certificate is the normalised Gibbs bound below (Theorem~2), which is stated directly in the logit gap $\Delta$ and is temperature-explicit. As a descriptive reading, the additive frontier still fixes a certified temperature $\tau^*=(\sstar)^2=d^2/(2\ln(A/\varepsilon))$ from the field's geometry. We test this on the real query/key vectors of every sampled head of two pretrained transformers---BERT-base and GPT-2---over long contexts ($\sim\!360$ keys per head). The identity is exact to the models' float32 precision ($<10^{-4}$ across all heads), and at $\tau^*$ the certified far-key interference tail stays within budget across $\sim\!35{,}000$ queries per model with \emph{zero} violations, overshooting by $18$--$33\times$ when $\tau$ is pushed to $4\tau^*$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:attention}). Attention's source weights $v_i$ are unbounded, so this is the interference-tail certificate (Proposition~\ref{prop:conserv}) rather than the full $V_{\max}=1$ locality readout, which transfers cleanly only when key norms are bounded---the pure-Gaussian case (the measured normalised far mass at $\tau^*$ is $0.35$ for BERT and $0.62$ for GPT-2, not $\varepsilon$-small, but well below the $0.80$--$0.84$ at $4\tau^*$). Reading attention through a kernel is not itself new---the polarisation view and the linear-attention/Performer line make the same identification~\cite{tsai2019,choromanski2021}; what is new here is that the label-free interference certificate transfers onto that kernel, giving attention a computable far-key guarantee it did not have before. One caveat on deployment: in a pretrained transformer $\tau$ is fixed at $\sqrt{d_k}$, so $\tau^*$ is the certified temperature the geometry \emph{implies}, and our BERT/GPT-2 numbers are a descriptive certification of that field, not a claim that the running model was retuned to $\tau^*$. The normalised-mass companion (Theorem~2) certifies the same heads without the unbounded-weight caveat: at the deployed $\tau=\sqrt{d_k}=8$ the measured normalised far mass is $0.24$--$0.27$, and the logit-gap temperature that would drive it below $\varepsilon$ is far cooler (median $\approx0.02$), quantifying exactly how much more peaked attention would have to be to be provably local---validated with zero violations across $\sim\!66{,}000$ query-heads on both models (SI Note~13).

\paragraph{From a single head to the full attention layer.} A single head is not the full attention layer. Multi-head attention runs $H$ heads in parallel, mixes each through a value matrix $W_V^h$, and combines them through an output projection $W_O$; the certificate must survive the combination of heads and projections---and it does, with a short argument. At $\tau^*$ each head's far keys carry normalised mass $m^h$ (the single-head far mass of Note~9); deleting the far set moves that head's output by at most $2m^h\lVert W_V^h\rVert_2 X$ ($X=\max_i\lVert x_i\rVert$), and stacking through $W_O$ gives $\lVert\Delta O\rVert_2\le\lVert W_O\rVert_2(\sum_h(2m^h\lVert W_V^h\rVert_2 X)^2)^{1/2}$ (Prop.~5, SI Note~11)---a layer-output locality bound computable from the pretrained weights and the per-head masses alone, no labels and no gradient. On a real \texttt{bert-base-uncased} layer ($H=12$) the bound holds at every sampled query across four layers, with the actual far-deletion perturbation a non-trivial $12$--$28\%$ of the layer output and the bound conservative by a measured $\sim\!50\times$. Honestly scoped: because attention's weights are unbounded the far masses are the measured $0.24$--$0.38$, not $\varepsilon$, so this certifies a \emph{bounded, computable} layer perturbation rather than an $\varepsilon$-small one---a label-free, geometry-only far-field guarantee for the full attention layer, loose by a measured factor but not vacuous.

\paragraph{The bounded-weight companion: prototypical networks.} Attention exercises the unbounded-weight edge of the class; a prototypical network~\cite{snell2017} exercises the clean interior. It classifies a query by $\mathrm{softmax}_k(-\lVert f(x)-c_k\rVert^2/\tau)$ over class prototypes $c_k$---already a Gaussian kernel field, with \emph{no} polarisation needed and all source weights $v_k=1$, so assumption (A1) holds with $V_{\max}=1$ exactly. Both the interference-tail certificate \emph{and} the full far-set locality readout (Corollary~\ref{cor:locality}) therefore transfer. We train a $4$-layer convolutional ProtoNet on Omniglot (test accuracy \SI{91.5}{\percent}) and certify its prototype fields on held-out $60$-way episodes ($12{,}000$ queries). On the \emph{unnormalised} field ($v_k=1$) the additive certificate applies at full strength: the far-prototype tail sits at $0.25\varepsilon$ with \emph{zero} violations, and the full far-set locality readout (Corollary~\ref{cor:locality}) transfers because the weights are bounded---the guarantee attention cannot get. The \emph{normalised} softmax probabilities are certified separately by the Gibbs theorem (Theorem~2), which is the correct object for a normalised readout and which we validate on this architecture in SI Note~13. Attention and prototypes thus bracket the two ends of the same class: attention's unbounded weights admit only the interference-tail bound, while the bounded-weight prototype additionally inherits the full locality readout.

\section{The budget travels: re-certification without labels}\label{sec:grow}
The certificate does something a labelled search structurally cannot: it re-certifies a field it has never labelled or tuned on. A labelled search spends its labels at tuning time, on the field it saw; once deployed on a field that has grown---as continually-updated models and growing memories do---it has no labels left to re-tune and no way to know whether its fixed bandwidth still holds the budget. Because $\sstar$ re-measures the mass $A$ from the field itself, it re-certifies for free. Re-measurement is what any label-free interference rule can do---a direct tail search would re-certify too (Section~\ref{sec:related})---but $\sstar$ carries an a-priori conservativeness proof into each new stage, which the empirical searches do not. This is not a higher score against a baseline; it is a capability the labelled-search category lacks, and the axis on which the static certificate is silent---so we give it equal weight.

The claim is a structural gap against a bandwidth frozen after one labelled tuning stage; the numbers measure it. We grow the field by density---from $3$ to $120$ examples per class ($300$ to $12{,}000$ centres)---on all three frozen backbones, fixing a labelled grid-search $\sigma$ once on the sparse initial field. As the field densifies, that frozen bandwidth becomes an over-smoother whose interference drifts out of budget while its accuracy stalls; $\sstar$ re-measures $A$, shrinks to track the density, and holds tail/$\varepsilon\!\approx\!0.1$ throughout (Fig.~\ref{fig:grow}). On ResNet-18 the frozen $\sigma=8.83$ climbs to tail/$\varepsilon=502$ and \SI{47.1}{\percent} accuracy while $\sstar$ reaches \SI{54.1}{\percent}---a $7$-point label-free margin; on ViT-B/16 the frozen tail reaches $3865\times$ budget and $\sstar$ leads by $6.1$ points. ResNet-50 is the honest boundary: its geometry keeps the frozen bandwidth near budget (tail/$\varepsilon$ peaks at $\approx\!2$, a mild crossing not a collapse), yet $\sstar$ still gains $3.2$ points. Safety-collapse rests on two backbones, the accuracy gain on all three.

Two guardrails, up front because promotion invites scrutiny. First, this is \emph{adaptivity, not dominance}: the margin is against a bandwidth \emph{deliberately frozen} on the sparse field. Against a per-stage re-tuned oracle $\sstar$ does not win; it \emph{tracks} it (within a fraction of a point on ResNet-18 and ViT, trailing by $\approx\!4$ on ResNet-50), label-free---its advantage is needing no labels to re-tune. Second, the win is \emph{regime- and mode-specific}: it appears in the dense, growing regime (at fixed low density $\sstar$ gives parity, below), and it follows \emph{densification}, not field size. A class-incremental run adding classes at fixed density ($10\!\to\!100$ classes, $120$ each) barely moves the geometry ($d$ drifts $-3.5\%$), so the frozen bandwidth stays in budget and ties $\sstar$---a clean null we report to fix the boundary. The certificate travels when local density changes, the growing-memory setting; it adds nothing when only the class count grows.

\paragraph{Public datasets.}\label{sec:public} On iris, wine, breast-cancer, and digits the naive bandwidth overshoots by \numrange{2.8}{147}$\times$ while $\sstar$ holds tail/$\varepsilon<1$. These fields are neither dense nor growing, so the labelled grid search is a fair fixed comparator, and $\sstar$ matches it within about two points on three of four (breast-cancer \SI{96.6}{} vs \SI{96.5}{}, digits \SI{97.0}{} vs \SI{97.0}{}, iris \SI{96.3}{} vs \SI{96.3}{\percent}); on wine it trails by \SI{2.2}{} points. $\sstar$ is a specificity-first bandwidth: at fixed low density it delivers parity, and in the dense, growing regime the guarantee turns into an accuracy win.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}
Density-estimation rules---plug-in bandwidths (Silverman, Scott~\cite{silverman1986,scott1992}), Sheather--Jones~\cite{sheather1991}, variable-bandwidth and diffusion selectors~\cite{abramson1982,terrell1992,botev2010}, Lepski's method~\cite{lepski1997}, and scale-space~\cite{lindeberg1994}---target an integrated squared error against a true \emph{density}; we target \emph{interference}, and derive the bandwidth from that objective using no labelled signal. Run on the three frozen backbones, these objective-mismatched selectors miss the budget in \emph{opposite} directions: Sheather--Jones under-smooths to a near-delta kernel that collapses to a $1$-nearest-neighbour rule (a numerically stable readout recovers its $1$-NN accuracy; the naive linear-domain evaluation underflows to chance, an artifact we avoid), while Gaussian-process marginal-likelihood over-smooths to $11$--$15\times\,\sstar$ (tail$/\varepsilon\sim3\times10^5$)---confirming that likelihood- and ISE-driven selection is blind to the interference budget in either direction. The label-free distance heuristics---the median heuristic~\cite{garreau2017} and local scaling~\cite{zelnikmanor2004}---fix the scale but not the safety factor, and overshoot the budget by roughly five orders of magnitude on the dense fields (Table~\ref{tab:multibb}). A sharper label-free comparator targets the interference directly: since $\mathrm{Tail}_\sigma(y,d)$ is monotone in $\sigma$ and costs the same kernel evaluations as measuring $A$, one can \emph{bisect} $\sigma$ until the measured mean tail equals $\varepsilon$---an empirical search for the same frontier, with no closed form and no proof. We run it on all three backbones. It is not free lunch: because $\sstar$ is provably conservative (its mean tail is only $0.08$--$0.16\,\varepsilon$), bisection deploys at a \emph{larger} bandwidth ($1.07$--$1.11\times\sstar$), and on these high-dimensional fields the labelled accuracy optimum lies \emph{below} $\sstar$, so moving up costs $0.8$--$2.1$ accuracy points rather than recovering them---bisection is further from the labelled grid than $\sstar$, not closer. More importantly it forfeits the guarantee: holding the \emph{mean} tail at $\varepsilon$ leaves the per-query distribution over budget (p99 tail$/\varepsilon$ $1.6$--$2.3$, max up to $2.8$), whereas the mean-$A$ form of $\sstar$ certifies the mean tail with an a-priori conservativeness proof, and its quantile-$A$ form holds every query below $\varepsilon$ by construction (SI Note~5), with the conformal wrapper (Proposition~7) supplying the finite-sample future-query guarantee. The distinction the paper claims is thus precise: $\sstar$ is a one-shot, closed-form, provably conservative certificate with an a-priori per-query form available; a label-free tail search is an empirical mean-fit with no closed form and, here, worse accuracy. Gaussian-process length scales (learned by the marginal-likelihood optimisation~\cite{gpml} measured above) have motivated uniform error bounds for safety-critical use~\cite{gp_safe2021}; $\sstar$ fixes the scale from geometry and offers an a priori certificate rather than a fitted posterior. Conformal prediction~\cite{vovk2005,angelopoulos2023} and selective classification~\cite{geifman2017} certify \emph{marginal} coverage from a \emph{labelled} calibration set, whereas Corollary~\ref{cor:floor} certifies \emph{per-query decision equality} without labels---both require a representative sample (label-free is not sample-free: $A$ is measured over held-out queries), and the two are complementary, a conformal wrapper calibrating the residual far-field probability our margin leaves uncontrolled. In continual learning~\cite{kirkpatrick2017,rebuffi2017}, where a correction field is adapted over a frozen model, the interference budget makes the stability--plasticity trade-off quantitative. The locality readout (Section~\ref{sec:cert}) relates to machine unlearning~\cite{ginart2019} and influence-based attribution~\cite{koh2017}: those quantify how removing a training point changes the output, while $\sstar$ \emph{certifies}, label-free and a priori, that removing any far point changes the output by at most $\varepsilon$ and corrupting it cannot flip a $4\varepsilon$-margin decision (Corollary~\ref{cor:locality})---a measured data-independence guarantee for distance-based predictors, distinct from the randomised, worst-case sensitivity of differential privacy.

\paragraph{Which models inherit the certificate.} The certificate uses only that the output is a weighted sum of training points with a distance-decaying weight, so it is a property of a \emph{family} of modern readouts, not of kernel density estimation alone. We demonstrate two members on real networks, chosen to bracket the class. Softmax attention reduces exactly to a Gaussian kernel field over keys with unbounded source weights; its certified far-key tail holds on BERT and GPT-2 (Fig.~\ref{fig:attention}). Prototypical networks are the bounded-weight companion ($v_k=1$), for which the full locality readout transfers, not just the tail; the certificate holds on a trained ProtoNet with an $\varepsilon$-small unnormalised far tail ($0.25\varepsilon$), and its normalised probabilities are certified by the Gibbs theorem (Fig.~\ref{fig:prototype}, Theorem~2). Between them they instantiate both ends of the assumption (A1). Two further families remain candidates we do \emph{not} test here: representer-point decompositions~\cite{yeh2018} write a network's logit as such a field, and a network near convergence acts as a kernel machine in its neural-tangent space~\cite{jacot2018}. For each, $\sstar$ would supply a candidate locality radius from the field's own geometry; verifying the budget on them is future work.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}
Three boundaries. On pure density recovery there is no near/far structure---every sample is near---so the separation objective is silent; the principle applies to fields that must \emph{discriminate}. $\sstar$ is specificity-first: its guarantee is on interference and accuracy dominance is regime-dependent (parity at fixed low density, an accuracy win only in the dense, growing regime). The re-certification advantage is likewise mode-specific---it follows density, not field size: a class-incremental run that adds classes at fixed density leaves the geometry, and a frozen bandwidth's budget, essentially unchanged (a null we report in Section~\ref{sec:grow}), so the growing-field claim is scoped to densification, the growing-memory regime, not to class count alone. And conservativeness (Proposition~\ref{prop:conserv}) is stated at fixed radius $d$; $d$ drifts by only $1\%$ over a $40\times$ densification, consistent with $O_p(n^{-1/2})$ quantile convergence (SI Note~5), so re-estimating it per stage corrects a vanishing term, but a field whose class-conditional geometry itself changes would need $d$ re-derived.

\section{Conclusion}
A kernel field has one honest bandwidth: the largest $\sigma$ a conservative envelope certifies to keep cross-source interference below a stated budget. It has a closed form, $\sstar=d/\sqrt{2\ln(A/\varepsilon)}$; it needs no labelled search because the mass $A$ is measured from the field; and it carries a closed-form certificate an empirical search does not---not only that the field is safe on average, but an a-priori per-query guarantee that every prediction it flags stable is decided by its intended sources. Because that mass is measured rather than tuned, the certificate does the one thing a labelled search structurally cannot: it re-certifies a field it has never labelled or tuned on. As the field densifies, the same label-free re-measurement tracks a per-stage re-tuned oracle on three backbones while a bandwidth frozen on the sparse early field drifts to hundreds of times the budget---the guarantee that a static, tuned choice cannot carry into the growing-memory regime where the labels are no longer there. And because the certificate needs only distance-decaying weights, it reaches objects never built as kernel fields: softmax attention and prototype networks inherit it---the full locality guarantee for the bounded-weight prototype, the interference bound for attention---putting a label-free locality guarantee on distance-based deep readouts, transformers included. The bandwidth question, for a kernel field, is not a search but a calibration: derive the certified bandwidth from geometry, and read off the guarantee. And because that certificate is differentiable and depends only on geometry, the natural next step is to make it a training objective---penalising the far tail, or clamping the bandwidth to $\sstar$, so a model is locality-certified by construction rather than after the fact; we leave that to future work.
